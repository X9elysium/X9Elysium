# The Almanack of Naval Ravikant — Deep Notes

**Author:** Eric Jorgenson (curating Naval Ravikant's writing & podcasts, 2007–2020)
**Year:** 2020 (free at navalmanack.com)
**Read time for this summary:** ~45 min
**Read time for the original book:** ~6 hrs (or much longer — it rewards re-reading)

> "Seek wealth, not money or status. Wealth is having assets that earn while you sleep. Money is how we transfer time and wealth. Status is your place in the social hierarchy."

The Almanack is not really a book — it's a curated collection of Naval's tweetstorms, podcast transcripts, and essays organized into two halves: **Wealth** (how to build it without getting lucky) and **Happiness** (how to be peaceful inside the building). Together they describe an operating system for a person who wants to earn and live well without becoming the thing that breaks people.

For a founder-led consulting business like X9Elysium, the Almanack is doing two jobs at once: it is the playbook for **how the founder structures time, leverage, and pricing**, and it is the antidote for **the burnout that kills most agency owners by year four**. Both are load-bearing. Skip either and the business dies.

---

## Part I — Wealth

### 1. Seek wealth, not money or status

Wealth is **assets that earn while you sleep**. Code that runs without you. Content that converts without you. Equity that compounds without you. Money is the IOU; status is the social game.

The killer line: *"You're not going to get rich renting out your time."* The hourly model is a wealth ceiling. Even at $500/hr, there are only so many hours, and the moment you stop, it stops.

**Implication:** A consulting business cannot be built purely on time-for-money. It must convert hours into reusable artefacts (frameworks, audits, content, code, playbooks, brand) that earn outside the hours. Naval's wealth is in equity. A boutique agency's wealth is in: (1) a brand that pulls inbound without paid ads, (2) repeatable IP that compresses delivery time per project, (3) a small founder-led roster of clients who refer more clients without being asked.

### 2. Specific knowledge

> "Specific knowledge is knowledge you cannot be trained for. If society can train you, it can train someone else and replace you."

You find specific knowledge by following genuine curiosity and obsession. It looks like play to you and looks like work to others. It cannot be taught in a school because it is not yet codified. By the time it is codified, it is no longer specific — it is commodity.

**Three tests for whether knowledge is specific:**
1. Does it feel like play to you and work to everyone else?
2. Could a bootcamp graduate replicate it after 12 weeks? If yes, not specific.
3. Does explaining it require unique stories and judgement, or can it be a Notion page? If a Notion page, not specific.

**Implication:** A senior Shopify Plus consultant who has personally migrated 30+ stores has specific knowledge. A junior who has read the docs does not. The founder-led wedge ("no juniors, no handoffs") is not a service quality slogan — it is an economic statement: the work being sold is specific knowledge, and specific knowledge does not survive being delegated downward.

### 3. The four kinds of leverage

> "Give me a lever long enough and a place to stand and I will move the earth." — Archimedes
>
> Modern levers, in increasing order of permissionlessness:
>
> 1. **Labour** — other people working for you. Oldest, most fragile, requires hierarchy.
> 2. **Capital** — money working for you. Permissioned (you have to convince someone with money).
> 3. **Code** — software that runs without supervision. Permissionless. Infinite copies at zero marginal cost.
> 4. **Media** — content that influences while you sleep. Permissionless. Compounds.

Naval's central wealth thesis: **code and media are the new leverage**. They are permissionless (no boss, no investor approval), and they have zero marginal cost of replication. Labour is bounded by the number of bodies. Capital is bounded by access. Code and media are bounded only by the quality of the idea.

**Implication for X9Elysium:**
- We choose code + media as primary leverage. The website, the llms.txt, the cornerstone content, the Instagram plan, the open-sourced frameworks — these are media. The reusable migration playbooks, the Shopify Plus accelerators, the audit scripts — these are code.
- We deliberately under-use labour. Two senior partners + frameworks beats 30 juniors + chaos. This is not a moral position; it is a leverage position.
- We do not raise capital. The agency is bootstrapped because outside capital comes with leverage of its own — over you. Naval's writing on this is unambiguous.

### 4. Productize yourself

> "Productize yourself. Take what is unique to you and what nobody else can do, and turn it into a product, into media, into a brand."

The founder is the product. Their judgement, their taste, their pattern library, their network, their voice. Everything downstream — the website, the case studies, the LinkedIn presence — is the wrapper that makes the founder accessible.

**Implication:** Darsh and Adhvait are the product at X9Elysium. The /about page rewrite (real LinkedIn-sourced bios, defensible four-year numbers, no fabricated milestones) is the productization. The /foundation page is the productization. The Instagram plan is the productization.

### 5. Play long-term games with long-term people

> "All the returns in life — wealth, relationships, knowledge — come from compound interest."

Compound interest only works if you stay in the same game with the same people for long enough. Switching games every 18 months resets the compounding. Switching people every project means trust never accumulates.

**Three signals you are in a long-term game:**
1. You can imagine working with this person again in 10 years and being glad you did.
2. The relationship survives a difficult conversation.
3. Both sides are willing to take a smaller short-term outcome to keep the relationship.

**Implication:** The Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam credo (one family, one outcome, one shared ledger of trust) is Naval's "long-term games with long-term people" rendered in Sanskrit. The Rule of True Partnership ("we say no to projects that don't align — long-term fit is a prerequisite, not a bonus") is the operating expression. Most agencies optimise for the next quarter; X9Elysium optimises for being the partner a retailer still has on speed-dial in 2036.

### 6. Earn with your mind, not your time

> "Become so good at what you do that the world has to seek you out, and pay you what you're worth — not what your hours are worth."

The hourly model is a tax on excellence. Two consultants doing the same task — one in 8 hours, one in 80 — get paid 10x apart for the same outcome. That is backwards. Pricing should reflect the value created, the scarcity of the judgement, the upside captured — not the clock.

**Implication:** X9Elysium does not publish a rate card. The contact page sidebar literally says "value scales with project size." Pricing is by scope, complexity, and upside — never by hour. This is Naval directly.

### 7. Escape competition through authenticity

> "No one can compete with you on being you. Most of life is a search for who and what needs you the most."

Competition kills margin. The way out is not to compete harder — it is to be different enough that the question of comparison stops making sense. You do this by leaning into the specific combination of skills, taste, and obsessions that no one else has.

**Implication:** "Founder-led Shopify Plus consulting in Canada with a Sanskrit credo and 10 published operating rules" is not a description anyone else can use without sounding ridiculous. It is escape velocity from comparison. The credo is the moat.

### 8. Reading, judgement, and the compounding mind

> "Read what you love until you love to read."

Naval reads at the rate of about a book a week. He is unusual among investors in that he reads almost no business books — instead he reads physics, philosophy, history, mathematics, and primary texts. The argument is that **first-principles thinking compounds, while business-book thinking goes stale in 18 months**.

**Implication:** This is why the foundation page cites the Maha Upanishad rather than HBR. It is also why this folder exists — to be the kind of curated library a serious operator builds for themselves, not the recycled stack of LinkedIn carousel summaries everyone else has read.

## Part II — Happiness

### 9. Happiness is a skill, not an outcome

> "Happiness is the absence of desire. The default state, when you remove the sensation of something being missing in your life, is happiness."

Naval reframes happiness from **achievement-pending** to **subtractive practice**. Most people think happiness arrives when X is achieved. Naval's frame: happiness is what is left when you stop generating new desires.

**The three layers:**
1. **Peace of mind** — calm even when external things shift.
2. **Peace of body** — physical health, sleep, sunlight, food.
3. **Peace of spirit** — alignment between values and actions.

**Implication:** Rule of Positivity Under Pressure. When something breaks at 11pm on a Friday, the reaction is solutions and energy — not blame, not drama. This is Naval's equanimity. It is also a competitive advantage: clients hire calm. Panic is contagious; so is composure.

### 10. Desire is a contract you make to be unhappy until you get it

This is the most useful sentence in the entire book for an operator under pressure.

Every desire — for the next contract, the next hire, the next milestone — is a self-imposed unhappiness with the present. Pick desires deliberately and few. Do not desire by default.

**Implication:** This is the operating principle behind "we say no to most inbounds." Every yes is a desire taken on. Every desire is a contract to be unhappy until it resolves. So we say yes to a small number of things that are worth being unhappy about, and no to everything else.

### 11. Free yourself from the social media of approval

Naval's harshest critique is reserved for the **status game** — Twitter, Instagram, LinkedIn likes, conference applause. Status is zero-sum. Wealth is positive-sum. Status games consume time and produce nothing durable.

**Implication:** The Instagram plan in `docs/marketing/instagram-content-plan.md` is structured around **DM-keyword CTAs that route to discovery calls**, not vanity metrics. The kill criterion is explicit: "zero qualified discovery calls attributed to IG by end of Month 2 = kill or rebuild the channel." This is Naval's frame applied — measure wealth (qualified leads), not status (likes).

### 12. The meaning-of-life question

> "We are here to do the work, and the work is the reward, and there is no other reward."

Naval's closing argument is not nihilistic — it is liberating. There is no destination. There is only the practice of doing well by people you respect, on problems you find interesting, in a way that compounds. That is the entire game.

**Implication:** This is the underlying reason X9Elysium exists. Not to flip in 5 years. Not to be acquired. Not to win an award. To do good work for retailers we respect, on problems we find interesting, in a way that compounds — for the next 20 years.

## What the book does NOT say (so we do not over-fit it)

Naval is a solo investor and writer. He is not a service business operator. The book is silent or weak on:

- **Team management at scale** — Naval's whole thesis is to avoid scale. If X9Elysium ever decides to hire beyond two senior partners, Naval's frame stops being load-bearing and Brad Smart / Patrick Lencioni / Andy Grove pick up the slack.
- **Sales** — Naval treats inbound demand as an output of being good. For a 4-year-old agency without 20 years of compounding behind it, that is not enough. We pair Naval with Thiel ("distribution > product when product is solved") to fill the gap.
- **Capital allocation inside a business** — Naval invests in companies; he does not run a P&L. For pricing, capacity, and reinvestment decisions inside the agency, we lean on Thiel (power law) and direct experience rather than Naval.
