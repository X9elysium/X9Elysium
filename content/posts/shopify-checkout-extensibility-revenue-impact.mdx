Shopify pulled the rug on `checkout.liquid` and a lot of merchants treated the migration as IT housekeeping. The merchants treating it as a CRO program are quietly pulling 5–15% AOV and 2–8% conversion ahead of competitors. This is the founder version: what the migration actually unlocks, where the money is, and how not to ruin your conversion rate doing it.

## Why this is a revenue conversation

For a decade Shopify Plus checkout customization happened in `checkout.liquid` — a single template file where developers could inject anything. It was flexible, fragile, and impossible to upgrade safely. Most heavy-customization merchants accumulated 200–800 lines of custom code in there over the years. Most of it was untested, undocumented, and broke quietly during BFCM.

The new model is different by design:

- **Checkout UI Extensions.** Small, focused components that render at specific extension points (banner above payment, after shipping options, post-purchase, etc).
- **Shopify Functions.** Server-side logic for things like custom discount rules, shipping rate selection, and payment method visibility.
- **Order Status / Thank-you page extensions.** Same model, applied to post-purchase.

Each piece is sandboxed. Each piece is upgrade-safe. Each piece gets independently observability. From a developer-experience standpoint this is night and day. From a *merchant* standpoint, the meaningful change is that you can finally run real CRO experiments inside checkout without everything breaking.

## The CRO surface that just opened up

For most of the last decade, the cart-to-checkout funnel was the least optimized part of e-commerce because experimenting there was scary. With extensibility, the testable surface multiplies:

**1. Post-purchase upsells.** The single highest-ROI CRO surface on Shopify Plus right now. Customer has paid. Their guard is down. A relevant one-click upsell adds 5–18% incremental AOV depending on category. Apps like ReConvert and AfterSell are mature on the new model. Don't run a Plus checkout without one.

**2. Trust elements based on cart contents.** Show shipping-protection messaging when the cart has fragile items. Show free-returns badges when the cart contains apparel. Show estimated delivery dates dynamically based on the customer's postal code. None of these were practical on `checkout.liquid` at scale.

**3. Smarter shipping presentation.** Sort shipping options by delivery date, not price. Pre-select the option with the best delivery promise relative to AOV. Hide options that won't make a promised date. Tested across DTC stores: 2–6% conversion lift from shipping UI alone.

**4. Loyalty point redemption inline.** Members redeem points without leaving checkout. Increases first-time-loyalty-redemption rate dramatically and lifts repeat-customer LTV.

**5. Gift options and address validation.** Easy to ship as extensions. Address autocomplete alone reduces shipping-address errors by 30–60%, which translates directly to fewer "package returned to sender" tickets and refunds.

**6. BNPL placement.** Affirm, Afterpay, Klarna at the *exact* moment a shopper sees their total — not buried below payment options. Smart placement lifts BNPL adoption by 20–40% on stores where BNPL is offered.

## Where merchants get the migration wrong

We've cleaned up enough botched migrations to see a pattern:

- **Treating it as code translation.** They move every old checkout customization line-for-line into the new model. They end up with a checkout that is exactly as cluttered, exactly as inconsistent, and exactly as un-tested. Migration is the moment to question every customization. Half of them shouldn't move.
- **Skipping the QA gauntlet.** New checkout architecture means new edge cases. Test gift-card-only checkouts, subscription orders, mixed cart types, every shipping zone, every payment method. The merchants that lost conversion in migration usually skipped this.
- **No A/B test post-launch.** Ship the new checkout, look at week-one numbers, panic if they dip. The right move is to keep both flows running for 1–2 weeks (Shopify supports gradual rollout), measure properly, then commit.
- **Forgetting the third-party app stack.** Subscriptions, custom fields, B2B tax exemption logic — every one of these touches checkout and may or may not have a Shopify-Functions equivalent. Audit before migration, not during.

## The migration roadmap that works

For a typical Plus store with 6–18 months of checkout.liquid history, the path:

**Week 1–2: Audit.**
- Document every line of custom checkout code.
- Map each customization to either: (a) keep as Checkout UI Extension, (b) keep as Shopify Function, (c) kill — never used.
- Audit third-party app dependencies.

**Week 3–6: Build.**
- Extensions for UI customizations (banners, trust badges, gift options).
- Functions for any custom discount, shipping, or payment logic.
- Wire post-purchase page.
- Replace third-party apps that haven't migrated to extensibility with apps that have.

**Week 7: Internal QA.**
- Every cart configuration, every shipping zone, every payment method, every coupon stack.
- Mobile and desktop. Apple Pay, Google Pay, Shop Pay. Subscription orders.

**Week 8: Gradual rollout.**
- Use Shopify's built-in rollout percentage to ramp the new checkout 10% → 25% → 50% → 100% over 7–10 days.
- Monitor conversion rate, AOV, and error rate at each step.
- Pull back if any of the metrics degrade by more than 2 standard deviations.

**Week 9 onward: CRO program.**
- A/B test post-purchase upsells.
- A/B test shipping presentation.
- A/B test trust elements based on cart contents.

## What the post-purchase upsell should look like

The single biggest win in the migration is the post-purchase upsell, and most merchants ship it badly. The pattern that works:

- **Single offer per upsell page.** Not a grid. Not three options. One product, one price, one CTA.
- **Discounted vs base price.** "Add this for $19 — normally $29." The discount is the reason this offer exists, post-purchase.
- **One-click acceptance.** No re-entering payment info. This is the killer feature — converts 4–10× higher than a re-checkout flow.
- **Relevant to the order.** Hat customer just bought a coat? Offer matching gloves. Sneakers? Offer socks. Use product recommendations data, not a flat default offer.
- **No more than two pages.** First page if accepted, second page as another offer. Third page is annoyance. Stop at two.

Stores running this pattern see post-purchase upsell take rates of 8–18% and AOV lifts of 6–14%. That's pure margin — no acquisition cost on this revenue.
