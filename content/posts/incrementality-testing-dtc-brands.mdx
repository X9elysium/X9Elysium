Every founder who's run paid ads at scale eventually realizes the dirty secret of digital advertising: most of what platforms report as "your ROAS" includes sales that would have happened anyway. The only honest way to know your true incremental contribution is to run a controlled experiment. This post is the founder-level explanation of how — and why DTC brands that run incrementality consistently outperform brands that don't.

## What "incremental" actually means

Imagine you spend $50,000 on Meta this month and Meta reports $200,000 in attributed revenue. That's a 4× ROAS. Sounds great.

Now imagine that of those $200,000 in attributed sales, $80,000 would have happened from email, organic search, returning customers, and word-of-mouth even if Meta didn't exist. The *incremental* contribution of Meta is $120,000, not $200,000. Your *incremental* ROAS is 2.4×, not 4×.

This is the difference between **attributed** and **incremental** ROAS, and it's the difference between brands that scale efficiently and brands that scale wastefully.

## Why platform-reported ROAS lies (in this direction)

Platforms credit themselves whenever any user touch happened on their platform within an attribution window. Meta credits 7-day-click and 1-day-view. Google credits last-click on Google Ads. TikTok credits 7-day-click. None of them know what *would have happened* without the ad.

The pattern shows up most aggressively on:

- **Brand search ads.** Customers searching your brand name were going to convert. The ad just intercepted the click.
- **Retargeting on existing customers.** Returning shoppers who would have come back via email or direct.
- **Lookalike audiences trained on customer lists.** Often serving ads to people already in your funnel through other channels.

A clean incrementality test is the only way to know what fraction of each channel is actually creating new demand.

## The four incrementality methods

**1. Geo holdout (gold standard).**
Pick matched regions. Run ads in some, pause ads in others, for 4 weeks. Compare per-capita sales. The difference is your incremental lift.

**2. Ghost campaigns / PSA holdouts (Meta-specific).**
Meta runs the ad but shows a placebo creative (PSA, brand awareness with no offer) to a randomized control group. Conversions in the control vs treatment groups gives you incremental lift. Meta supports this through its Conversion Lift Studies tool — useful for $100K+ monthly spend brands.

**3. Synthetic controls (econometric).**
Use historical sales data to model what *should have* happened. Compare to what *did* happen. Useful for back-testing or when geo holdouts aren't feasible. Less robust than geo holdouts but cheaper.

**4. Quasi-experimental (events-based).**
Tracking sales around natural ad pauses (out of stock, account suspension, budget cutoff). Less controlled but free signal — every brand has these data points and most don't analyze them.

For most DTC brands at $30K–$500K/month, **geo holdouts are the right starting point.** Below $30K/month spend, synthetic controls are more practical.

## How to design a geo-holdout test

The mechanics:

**Step 1: Pick matched markets.**
You need pairs of regions with similar demographics, sales volume, and seasonality. For Canadian DTC brands, common pairings:

- Toronto vs Vancouver
- Calgary vs Edmonton
- Halifax vs Quebec City (if French-language friendly)
- US: Chicago vs Houston, Phoenix vs Denver

**Step 2: Run a pre-period.**
Two weeks of "normal" ad activity in both treatment and control regions. Establish baseline performance ratios.

**Step 3: Treat.**
Pause ads in the holdout regions for 4 weeks. Continue normal spend in treatment regions. Don't change anything else mid-test.

**Step 4: Measure.**
Compare per-capita revenue change in holdout vs treatment regions. Account for any seasonality or external factors. The delta is your incremental lift.

**Step 5: Calculate true ROAS.**
Apply the lift ratio to your platform-reported ROAS. Real incremental ROAS = Platform-reported ROAS × (Incremental lift / Total attributed revenue).

## Common pitfalls

**Test power.** If you don't have enough sales volume in the holdout regions, the test produces a wide confidence interval. The fix is either bigger spend, longer test, or larger geographic regions.

**Bleed-over.** TV-style brand awareness can create demand in your holdout regions that has nothing to do with the test channel. A simultaneous OOH campaign in the holdout regions invalidates the geo-holdout for digital.

**Seasonality changes.** A test running through a major shopping moment (Mother's Day, a major sale) gets distorted. Avoid testing through peak weeks.

**Budget reshuffling.** When you pause Meta in a region, you can't redeploy that budget to Google in the same region — that contaminates the test. Hold the entire ad budget constant in the holdout region.

## What we typically find

Across geo-holdout tests we've run for DTC brands in the last 24 months, the incrementality patterns:

| Channel | Typical platform ROAS | Typical incrementality | True incremental ROAS |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Meta cold prospecting | 2.0–3.0x | 60–85% | 1.4–2.4x |
| Meta retargeting | 6.0–12.0x | 15–35% | 1.0–3.5x |
| Google brand search | 15–40x | 10–30% | 2.0–10x |
| Google non-brand search | 3.0–6.0x | 70–90% | 2.5–5.0x |
| TikTok cold prospecting | 2.0–4.0x | 65–90% | 1.4–3.5x |
| Pinterest | 3.0–5.0x | 50–80% | 1.7–4.0x |
| Email (existing list) | 30–60x | 30–60% | 12–35x |

The biggest surprise for most founders: **brand search ads are 70–90% non-incremental.** People typing your brand name were going to find you. The ad just charged you for the click. Many brands cut brand-search spend by 50–70% after their first test and see negligible total revenue impact.

The flip side: **cold prospecting on Meta and TikTok is more incremental than the platform-reported ROAS suggests** — because those platforms also under-report iOS conversions. Sometimes a channel that looks like a 2× platform ROAS turns out to be a 2× *incremental* ROAS, which is a great deal.

## When not to bother

Incrementality testing is expensive — both in foregone sales during holdouts and in analyst time. It's the wrong investment when:

- Total monthly ad spend is below $30K (insufficient signal).
- The brand is in early growth / launch mode where every dollar is exploratory anyway.
- The brand has only one paid channel (no allocation decisions to make).
- A major channel mix change is already planned for other reasons.

It's the *right* investment when:

- Multi-channel ad spend at $50K+/month with allocation decisions.
- Considering scaling a single channel materially (2×+ budget increase).
- Reconciliation discrepancies between platform reports and P&L are persistent.
- The team is making strategic budget decisions for the next quarter or year.
